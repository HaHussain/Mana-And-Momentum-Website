Travel is the process of going to another [[Locations|Location]].

Travel out into the wilds can occur as a hex-crawl or point-crawl while travel in urban centres can largely be skipped over unless there is potential danger.
For smaller local maps, 1 hex represents a distance of 5 or 10km, where each hex is 1 or 2 hours of travel at standard walking speed respectively.
For larger maps, 1 hex represents 40km, where each hex is 1 day of travel at standard speed by a starting [[Fortress]].

When starting out on a journey, the players choose a [[#Travel Speed]], a route and assign [[#Travel Duties]].
Then each travel period each [[Rules/Characters/index|Character]] assigned to a Duty, rolls, discusses with their group, assigns what they were able to perform with their duty and evaluates their results.


# Travel Speed

When beginning travel and at the start of the next travel period, the players may select a travel speed that effects how fast they travel distances, and how easy they are to track.

Fast - [[Exert]] daily for : 2x Speed, 4 ticks on the [[#Danger Clock]] per day
Standard - 1x Speed, 2 ticks on the [[#Danger Clock]] per day
Slow - 0.5x Speed, 1 tick on the [[#Danger Clock]] per day

The slower the players move, the more effect their [[#Travel Duties]] have, especially [[#Handling]] and [[#Foraging]].

If the players do not use a [[Fortress]], they cover half their usual distance (20km a day - 4 ticks on the [[#Travel Clock]]) due to the weight of their equipment, unless they all reduce their [[Weight]] to their [[Strength|STR]] or less.

## Paths

When players take a route between two hexes that has a path, they cannot get lost and move at 2x Speed.


## Travel Clock
The Travel Clock is an 8-[[Clock]] that tracks extra travel on a large map that doesn't fit neatly into 1 hex of travel.
Each clock tick represent 5km of movement.

For every extra 1m of Speed the [[Fortress]] has, it moves an additional 5km per day - 1 tick on the Travel Clock.

The Travel Clock is wiped when the players reach a [[Locations|Location]], and can be used to represent the time of day they got there at.

### Travelling Extra
On a small map every 7m of Speed (Rounded Down), the [[Fortress]] moves 5km per hour - 1 hex of travel.
%% 7m/10s (SPEED) / 10 (Convert m/10s to m/s) * 2 (2 actions per turn) * 3.6 (m/s to km/h) = 5.04km/h %%

On a large map, players can [[Exert]] their steeds (or themselves) to force march and travel for an additional hour.



## Danger Clock

The Danger Clock is an 8-[[Clock]] that represents the danger of travel, whether that's the animosity of nature, the distance of the guards tracking down the party, or how close the dragon overhead is to spotting them. 
Each travel day increases the danger clock by some amount (2 ticks by default), when it ticks up, some number of enemies are added to the Danger Clock's enemy pool. When the clock completes, the characters are forced into an encounter with the entire enemy pool.




# Travel Duties

When exploring out in the wild, there are many important tasks that must be handled to not die in the wilds.

Navigating - The process of ensuring you're on track to your destination and knowing where you are.
Scouting - Discovering the area around you.
Foraging - Looking for food and water and other useful items.
Handling - Managing the pace of animals and driving of the [[Fortress]].


## Navigating
Navigating helps ensure the party is on track and heading in the correct direction.
%%The Purpose of navigating is to ensure that it makes sense that mountains block travel, and people use roads, making journeying without following roads and markers more difficult. Players should feel like heading away from the roads innovative but scarier.

For a player - navigating is about making choices where to go, a skilled navigator knows how to create paths through terrain that is normally impassible while unskilled navigators have to stick to roads.%%
Navigation is an [[Awareness]] / [[Intelligence]] : [[Traverse]] roll, for every 5 above 10, you gain 1 Navigation point that must be used within that travel time period. Navigation points can be spent to:
- x points to not get lost depending on the terrain. If you do not spend points to do this, then you move into a random adjacent hex instead.
- 2 points to permanently add this hex move as a [[#Paths|Path]].
- 1 point to trigger this terrain's unique feature.
- 1 point to give any other role 2 points.


## Scouting
Scouting is an [[Awareness]] : [[Search]] roll, for every 5 above 10, you gain 1 Scouting point that must be used within that travel time period. 
Scouting points can be spent to:
- 1 point to select an adjacent hex and reveal it. (Repeatable)
- 2 points to select a hex [[Range]] 2 away, and reveal it. (Repeatable)
	- And so on...
- 1 point to know if the danger clock is above half or not.
- 2 points to know the exact value of the danger clock.
- 2 points to know the danger the danger clock represents.
Scouting helps discover the interesting things around the party as they travel and stops the dangerous things from attacking the party as they travel.
%%Scouting gives the party the opportunity to discover things about the world and its culture as well as subplots. Monsters aim to make the outskirts feel populated with living breathing beasts, many of which are dangerous.
In general when the party sets out on a journey, you can decide is this a moment for attrition or tension.
If the players are heading from one safe location to another, there is generally no point for attrition unless it is a long journey, where the likelihood of survival is in question.

In a scene where the goal is attrition - the goal is to have cool and interesting scenarios that drain the player's resources. And an interesting fight, like an ambush or some other people being attacked creates the opportunity of gaining some lore for their actions.
You can start this combat at any time, but do note, too much will slow down the pace of the story and cause disinterest so do this when it serves the story.

In a scene where the goal is tension - the goal is to immediately have a cool scene where there is a chance of failure.

For a player - scouting is about knowing what issues are around you, not being surprised by monsters, finding cool things in the distance.%%
Scouting is an [[Awareness]] roll, that can get more difficult if the terrain block line of sight.


## Foraging
Foraging is an [[Instinct]] : [[Nature]] roll, for every 5 above 10, you gain 1 Foraging point that must be used within that travel time period. 
Foraging points can be spent to:
- 1 point to feed a [[Rules/Characters/index|Character]]. (Repeatable)
- 1 point to allow another character with another exploration role to reroll their exploration roll. (Repeatable)
- 1 point to restore 1 [[Item Points|Item Point]] for a character or the Fortress. 
- 1 point to uncover a rare resource of the area.
If a character is not fed that day then they must either take a point of [[Exert#Exertion|Exertion]] or take 10 [[Health Points|HP]] damage.
%%Foraging showcases that people cannot exist out in the wilderness forever, that it takes a toll on people.

For a GM - Foraging allows you to reduce the player's resources over time, forcing them to only travel short distances, rest when they get to a scene, and if they're forced to travel long distance, it threatens them by eating up their resources.

For a Player - Foraging fuels the other characters on the journey, by gathering more, you are able to save and enhance other characters, their successes become yours. By succeeding with Foraging, you can journey further, in more treacherous terrain, have more resources later, and give other players more chances to succeed. Likewise by failing with Foraging, your parties resources begin to drain forcing them to retreat back sooner.%%


## Handling
Handling is an [[Instinct]] : [[Arcana]] / [[Nature]] / [[Society]] / [[Tech]] roll (depending on steeds), for every 5 above 10, you gain 1 Handling points that must be used within that travel time period. 
Handling points can be spent to:
- Add 1m of Speed to the [[Fortress]] per point.
- Reduce the danger clock by 1 per point.
%%Handling is the smaller scale process of actually moving, handling the [[Fortress]] and its animals.

For a GM - Handling lets you throw combat encounters towards the players at their own pace, keeping the pressure on them but letting them handle it. If they focus on moving fast they may get attacked by monsters and be cockier, if they focus on moving stealthily, they rely on their forager more as their resources are drained.

For a Player - Being good at handling lets you cover distances quicker or avoid combat encounters or both, whilst being bad at it will make it tougher to move.%%
